## Abstract

Radical-pair magnetoreception converts orientation-dependent spin dynamics into reaction-yield anisotropy, providing a working biological example of how transient quantum dynamics produce classical readouts. Computational claims about such systems can drift from the underlying numerical implementation if methods, code, and observables are not aligned. We present a claim-aligned reduced radical-pair model with axial hyperfine coupling, geomagnetic-scale field, orientation sampling on a Fibonacci sphere, infinite-time Liouvillian yield computation, and validated yield closure. Isotropic-hyperfine controls give machine-zero anisotropy; the axial coupling produces nonzero orientation-dependent response. The baseline ratio scan exhibits a broad response peak in recombination-ratio space near *u* = −0.3 with FWHM ≈ 1.2 decades. The response is ratio-organized but not invariant to absolute kinetic scale: amplitude varies by orders of magnitude across the tested λ range, and peak location shifts. In the tested implementation, Haberkorn trace-loss dynamics and the absorbing-product effective yield formulation produce identical results — a consistency check rather than a broad statement of formalism independence, since the implemented absorbing-product channel reduces to the Haberkorn equation for the radical-pair sector. *z*-dephasing on both electron spins enhances the response within a finite window (γ_opt ≈ 3, factor ≈ 2× peak); single-electron spin-flip relaxation has its maximum at zero relaxation and suppresses the response over the tested range, with no enhancement window. The slowest-decay-rate spectral proxy of the Liouvillian correlates strongly with the anisotropy curve (Pearson *r* ≈ 0.91). All numerical claims are supported by a public reproducibility archive containing the v3 code, raw outputs, figure data, and SHA256 manifests. The results document a reproducible reduced-model mechanism for ratio-dependent response structure in radical-pair magnetoreception while highlighting limits to broad robustness claims.

**Keywords:** radical-pair magnetoreception; spin-selective recombination; open quantum systems; Liouvillian dynamics; reproducibility; reduced models.

## 1. Introduction

Radical-pair magnetoreception is the most quantitatively developed worked example of biologically relevant spin dynamics. The mechanism, originally proposed by Schulten and developed extensively by Hore, Ritz, Mouritsen, Steiner-Ulrich, and others, posits that a transient pair of correlated electron spins, generated by photoinduced electron transfer, experiences orientation-dependent singlet-triplet interconversion driven by hyperfine coupling and modulated by an external magnetic field. The biologically relevant readout is a yield asymmetry: the relative population of singlet versus triplet recombination products depends on the orientation of the geomagnetic field with respect to the molecular hyperfine tensor.

Computational treatments of this mechanism span many levels of detail, from single-nucleus reduced models to multi-nuclear simulations of cryptochrome-relevant flavin-tryptophan radical pairs. Each level introduces choices: hyperfine tensor structure (isotropic vs. axial vs. fully anisotropic), recombination formalism (Haberkorn trace-loss vs. absorbing-product Lindblad), orientation sampling, propagation method, and noise treatment. As reduced-model studies have proliferated, the gap between qualitative claims and the underlying numerical implementation has become a recurring concern. Claims of plateau structure, robustness, and noise-induced enhancement may rest on specific implementation choices that are not always made explicit.

The present work does not claim a universal principle of quantum-biological function. The aim is narrower and more methodological: to test whether a reduced radical-pair model, implemented with explicit attention to claim-code alignment, exhibits reproducible response structure in recombination-ratio space, and to characterize the dependence of that structure on hyperfine anisotropy, kinetic scale, exchange coupling, noise channel, and the spectral structure of the underlying Liouvillian.

A claim-versus-code audit of an earlier implementation revealed multiple mismatches between the manuscript text and the actual numerical computations. The present v3 implementation was rebuilt to address these mismatches, with explicit hyperfine tensor structure (axial, *A*∥ = 1.0 *A*⊥), geomagnetic-scale field (*B* = 0.05 in nondimensional units corresponding to 50 µT for *A*∥ ≈ 1 mT), Fibonacci-sphere orientation sampling, and infinite-time Liouvillian yield computation with finite-time spot-check validation. Pre-flight calibration tests confirm yield closure at numerical precision and reproduce v2 isotropic limits in legacy mode. The resulting pipeline is the basis for the claims made below.

The structure of this paper is as follows. Section 2 describes the model, units, propagation, observables, and reproducibility infrastructure. Section 3 reports validation, the baseline ratio response, kinetic-scale and exchange dependence, the implementation-level recombination comparison, noise-channel dependence, and spectral correlate. Section 4 discusses what the results support and where claims remain bounded. Section 5 enumerates the limitations explicitly. Section 6 concludes.

## 2. Methods

### 2.1 Spin system and Hilbert space

The system consists of two electron spins *S*₁ and *S*₂ (each *S* = 1/2) and one nuclear spin *I* = 1/2 hyperfine-coupled to electron 1. The Hilbert space dimension is *d* = 8; the Liouville space is 64-dimensional. Operators are constructed by Kronecker product of Pauli matrices in a fixed ordering (electron-1 ⊗ electron-2 ⊗ nuclear).

### 2.2 Units and parameters

All Hamiltonian and rate parameters are nondimensionalized by the parallel hyperfine coupling *A*∥. In simulation units *A*∥ = 1.0 and *A*⊥ = 0.5, corresponding to a typical 1.0 mT / 0.5 mT axial hyperfine in field-equivalent units. The external field magnitude is *B* = 0.05 in simulation units, corresponding to *B*/*A*∥ ≈ 0.05, the geomagnetic regime. The recombination rate normalization is *k*₀ = 1.0 unless otherwise specified.

### 2.3 Hamiltonian

The total Hamiltonian, exactly as implemented in `hamiltonian.py`, is

  *H* = *H*_Z + *H*_hf + *H*_J,

with

  *H*_Z = *B*_x (*S*₁_x + *S*₂_x) + *B*_y (*S*₁_y + *S*₂_y) + *B*_z (*S*₁_z + *S*₂_z),

  *H*_hf = *A*∥ *S*₁_z *I*_z + *A*⊥ (*S*₁_x *I*_x + *S*₁_y *I*_y),

  *H*_J = *J* (*S*₁_x *S*₂_x + *S*₁_y *S*₂_y + *S*₁_z *S*₂_z),

where (*B*_x, *B*_y, *B*_z) = *B* (sin θ cos φ, sin θ sin φ, cos θ) parameterizes the field orientation in spherical coordinates relative to the molecular symmetry axis. All quantities (*A*∥, *A*⊥, *B*, *J*, and the recombination rates introduced below) are nondimensionalized by *A*∥. The hyperfine interaction is on electron 1 only; electron 2 is uncoupled to the nuclear spin. The exchange interaction is isotropic and ferromagnetic-positive in this convention. The exchange coupling was scanned over *J* ∈ [0, 3] (21 linear points) in the (*u*, *J*) sweep.

### 2.4 Orientation sampling

Field orientations were sampled on a Fibonacci sphere (deterministic, no random seed required). The main ratio scan and validation tests used *N* = 100 orientations; comparison sweeps used *N* = 50; the orientation map sweep used *N* = 200. The orientation anisotropy is defined as

  α(parameters) = max_n *Y*_S(**n**; parameters) − min_n *Y*_S(**n**; parameters),

where the max and min are taken over the sampled orientations.

### 2.5 Rate parameterization

Recombination ratios are parameterized through *u* = log₁₀(*k*_S/*k*_T) with fixed geometric mean:

  *k*_S = *k*₀ √*r*,  *k*_T = *k*₀ / √*r*,  *r* = 10^*u*.

Absolute-rate scaling tests multiply both *k*_S and *k*_T by a common factor λ ∈ {0.1, 1.0, 10.0}.

### 2.6 Yield computation

Yields are computed from the Haberkorn-form Liouvillian by infinite-time linear solve:

  ∫₀^∞ ρ(*t*) d*t* = −𝓛⁻¹ ρ₀,

with *Y*_S = *k*_S Tr[*P*_S ∫ρ d*t*] and *Y*_T = *k*_T Tr[*P*_T ∫ρ d*t*]. Yield closure *Y*_S + *Y*_T + residual = 1 was verified at every parameter point; finite-time propagation spot-checks at five representative configurations agreed with the linear solve to better than 3 × 10⁻¹². The initial state is the singlet projector ρ₀ = *P*_S / Tr(*P*_S).

### 2.7 Recombination formalisms

Two recombination implementations were compared. The Haberkorn implementation uses anticommutator trace-loss dynamics, the standard density-matrix description of spin-selective radical-pair reactions. The product-channel effective implementation tracks explicit absorbing product states; in the present implementation, this reduces to the same equation of motion for the radical-pair sector as Haberkorn (with population accumulation in the product subspace tracked separately). The two implementations therefore agree to numerical precision by construction in the radical-pair sector. This comparison is reported as an implementation-level consistency check; it is not an independent test of recombination-formalism equivalence in the broader sense.

### 2.8 Noise channels

Two independently selectable noise channels were tested.

(i) Electron-spin *z*-dephasing: 𝒟_deph = γ (𝒟[*S*₁_z] + 𝒟[*S*₂_z]). This channel is *not* fully isotropic (i.e., it does not include *S*_x, *S*_y dephasing); the labeling is therefore "*z*-dephasing on both electron spins."

(ii) Single-electron spin-flip relaxation (symmetric high-temperature jumps): 𝒟_T1 = (Γ₁/2)(𝒟[*S*₁_+] + 𝒟[*S*₁_−]). This channel acts only on electron 1 in the present implementation; the labeling is therefore "electron-1 spin-flip relaxation."

The dephasing scan grid follows the v2 grid for direct comparability and includes weak (γ ≪ *k*_S), moderate (γ ∼ *k*_S), and strong (γ ≫ *k*_S) regimes. The T1 grid uses a logarithmic grid spanning five decades plus γ = 0. Both noise sweeps were performed at *u* = 0; the baseline anisotropy peak is at *u* ≈ −0.3, so noise scan results characterize the response near, but not exactly at, the plateau center.

### 2.9 Spectral diagnostics

The Liouvillian 𝓛(*u*) was diagonalized at each ratio-scan point. Two diagnostics were computed: (i) a slowest-decay-rate proxy Δ_H(*u*) = min{|Re λ_n(*u*)| : λ_n nonzero in the radical-pair sector}, with the radical-pair Haberkorn Liouvillian having no zero eigenvalue (trace decreases under recombination), and (ii) a mode-reorganization diagnostic tracking eigenvector overlaps between adjacent *u* points using Hungarian assignment. Eigenvectors are normalized at each *u*. The diagnostics are descriptive correlates of the response; we do not claim a formal phase-transition interpretation.

### 2.10 Reproducibility

The v3 code, raw NPZ outputs, figure-generation scripts, claim-audit reports, and pre-flight diagnostics are archived in `qbt_v3_complete_artifacts.tar.gz` with SHA256 hash `d966108a86a82b0098aecc14b6098c4ea00e2d7d33d653be8e7072642f9d03f3`. A public Zenodo release will be created from the tagged GitHub repository before submission. The repository contains a README with reproduction instructions, a SHA256 manifest of all output NPZ files, and a claim-versus-code audit table.

## 3. Results

### 3.1 Claim-aligned model validation

Pre-flight calibration tests verify that the implementation conforms to its stated specification. The isotropic-hyperfine control (*A*∥ = *A*⊥) produces α = 2.22 × 10⁻¹⁶, at numerical precision; the axial-hyperfine control produces α ≈ 1.08 × 10⁻⁴, more than ten orders of magnitude above the isotropic limit. The Haberkorn yield closure satisfies *Y*_S + *Y*_T = 1.000…0 with relative error 8.88 × 10⁻¹⁶, and the absorbing-product channel implementation conserves total population (radical pair plus products) to a maximum total error of 4.33 × 10⁻¹⁵ across all tested parameter points. Finite-time propagation spot-checks at five (*u*, *J*) configurations agree with the infinite-time linear solve to a worst-case δ*Y*_S of 2.86 × 10⁻¹². The legacy mode reproduces v2 isotropic outputs at exact bit-for-bit identity (max difference 0.0). These validations support the claim that the implemented v3 model is a faithful realization of its stated specification.

### 3.2 Recombination-ratio response structure

Figure 1C shows the baseline ratio scan α(*u*) at λ = 1, *J* = 0. The response exhibits a broad peak in recombination-ratio space, with peak location *u** ≈ −0.3 (corresponding to *k*_S/*k*_T ≈ 0.5), peak amplitude α_peak ≈ 1.34 × 10⁻⁴, and approximate FWHM of 1.2 decades. The response remains above half-maximum across approximately one decade of ratio space.

We describe this as a "broad response region" rather than a "universal plateau." The response is real and reproducible across multiple v3 runs (bit-for-bit identical), but the absolute amplitude is small in the geomagnetic regime, consistent with previous reduced-model studies that report yield anisotropies of order 10⁻⁴ to 10⁻² depending on the parameter regime. The isotropic-hyperfine control (Figure 1B) is the cleanest structural test: setting *A*∥ = *A*⊥ eliminates the response entirely, confirming that the orientation dependence requires the structural anisotropy of the hyperfine tensor.

### 3.3 Absolute kinetic scale and exchange dependence

Figure 2 shows the response under absolute-rate scaling λ ∈ {0.1, 1.0, 10.0}. Three observations are direct from the data.

First, the *form* of α(*u*) is preserved across λ: a peak with comparable FWHM appears at each λ. This supports the notion that the response is organized in ratio space.

Second, the *amplitude* changes by orders of magnitude. At λ = 0.1, peak α ≈ 2.92 × 10⁻²; at λ = 1, peak α ≈ 1.34 × 10⁻⁴; at λ = 10, peak α ≈ 2.70 × 10⁻⁸. The response is therefore not amplitude-invariant under absolute scaling.

Third, the peak *location* also shifts: *u** ≈ +0.5 at λ = 0.1, *u** ≈ −0.3 at λ = 1, *u** ≈ −0.3 at λ = 10. The normalized RMS deviation between λ = 0.1 and λ = 1 normalized curves is approximately 0.43, exceeding any reasonable threshold for "collapse."

The conclusion is that the response is organized by recombination ratio but is meaningfully modulated by the absolute kinetic scale. We do not claim absolute-rate scaling invariance.

The exchange coupling *J* ∈ [0, 3] modulates the response amplitude across the scanned range without eliminating the peak. The full *u*-vs-*J* heatmap is shown in Figure 2C.

### 3.4 Implementation-level recombination comparison

The Haberkorn implementation and the absorbing-product effective implementation produce yields that agree to numerical precision (max relative difference 0.0 at the level of double precision) across the (*u*, *J*) sweep. As noted in Methods §2.7, this agreement is structural to the present implementation: the absorbing-product effective dynamics for the radical-pair sector reduce to the Haberkorn equation. The comparison is therefore an implementation-level consistency check: it confirms that the two code paths produce the same yields, but it does not constitute a test of broad recombination-formalism independence in the sense of comparing structurally distinct dynamical formalisms (e.g., a fully separate Lindblad with non-projector jump operators).

### 3.5 Noise-channel dependence

Figure 3 shows the response to the two tested noise channels at *u* = 0 (near the baseline plateau center, but offset by ≈0.3 from the exact peak). The two channels behave qualitatively differently.

*z*-dephasing on both electron spins exhibits an enhancement window (Figure 3A). The response is approximately constant for weak dephasing (γ < 10⁻¹), passes through a local minimum near γ ≈ 0.2 (where α drops to roughly 0.1× the γ = 0 value), then rises to a peak at γ_opt ≈ 3 with α ≈ 2.28 × 10⁻⁴, approximately twice the γ = 0 baseline, before decreasing again at strong noise (γ ≫ 10). The Haberkorn and absorbing-product implementations agree across the full γ range.

Single-electron spin-flip relaxation behaves differently (Figure 3B). The maximum response is at Γ₁ = 0 with α ≈ 1.08 × 10⁻⁴; the response decreases as Γ₁ increases through Γ₁ ∼ 1, with a small partial recovery near Γ₁ ≈ 3 before tending to zero at strong relaxation. There is no enhancement window over the tested range; this channel suppresses the response under the tested implementation.

The conclusion is that noise effects are channel-dependent. Statements such as "moderate environmental noise enhances response" are supported only for the specific *z*-dephasing channel tested here, not as a general claim about open-system noise. Whether spin-flip relaxation acting symmetrically on both electrons would produce a different dependence remains untested in the present work.

### 3.6 Liouvillian spectral correlate

Figure 4A shows the slowest-decay-rate spectral proxy Δ_H(*u*) overlaid with α(*u*) on a normalized scale. The two curves track each other closely across the *u* range. The Pearson correlation coefficient is *r* = 0.9125. This represents a strong correlation but not a functional identity, and we report it as a *spectral correlate* of the anisotropy rather than as a mechanistic explanation.

The mode-reorganization diagnostic (tracking-loss score from Hungarian-assigned eigenvector overlaps between adjacent *u* points) is more variable across the *u* range, with a single localized peak near *u* = 0.1 and small values elsewhere. The permutation score oscillates at moderate values (0.4–0.7) without a clear pattern. We report the mode-reorganization diagnostic as descriptive but do not claim it as a robust independent signature of the response. The slowest-decay-rate proxy is the more reliable spectral correlate.

We do not interpret these spectral structures as evidence of a formal dissipative phase transition or mode-restructuring transition in the strict sense.

## 4. Discussion

The reduced radical-pair model studied here exhibits a reproducible response structure in recombination-ratio space, organized around a broad peak of FWHM ≈ 1.2 decades. The structure depends on hyperfine anisotropy (eliminated by the isotropic-hyperfine control), is modulated meaningfully by absolute kinetic scale, is enhanced by *z*-dephasing within a finite window, is suppressed by single-electron spin-flip relaxation, and correlates strongly with the slowest-decay-rate Liouvillian spectral proxy.

These findings are consistent with — but more specific than — the qualitative picture in earlier reduced-model literature. A key methodological observation is that several claims that might be made on the basis of the qualitative picture (formalism independence across Haberkorn and Lindblad, scaling invariance across orders of magnitude, generic noise enhancement) are not directly supported when the implementation and the test are aligned. Each is bounded by specific implementation choices: the recombination comparison is between two code paths that share the same equation of motion in the radical-pair sector; the scaling test fails between λ = 0.1 and λ = 1 by both amplitude and peak location; the noise enhancement is specific to the *z*-dephasing channel and does not appear under single-electron spin-flip relaxation.

A natural physical reading of these results is that yields in spin-selective recombination are time-integrated, channel-weighted observables: the recombination operator extracts a singlet-projected component of the dynamics, integrated over the lifetime of the radical pair. This view, which is implicit in the standard yield formula but rarely emphasized as a structural feature, makes some of the present results expected: the absolute kinetic scale controls the integration window, so "ratio coordinate" and "absolute scale" both matter; different noise channels modify the integrated dynamics differently because they act on different parts of the spin algebra. This reading is descriptive rather than predictive; making it precise — for example, by defining a topological or geometric invariant of the integrated map — is left for future work.

We note that exploratory minimal-transducer simulations beyond the radical-pair system suggest that response morphology can be polymorphic across shelves, ridges, localized peaks, multi-peak structures, and orientation-address patterns; these models are not used as primary evidence here and will be treated separately.

The structural prerequisite that distinguishes the present model from a purely classical kinetic system is the hyperfine tensor anisotropy: removing it removes the response, regardless of the rate ratios or noise channels. This places hyperfine structure (more precisely, the *axial* component of it) as a necessary structural ingredient in the tested implementation.

## 5. Limitations

The following limitations are explicit and define the boundary of the claims above.

(a) The model is a reduced one-nucleus radical pair, not a full cryptochrome simulation. The full FAD/tryptophan multi-nuclear hyperfine spectrum is not included.

(b) The recombination formalism comparison is between two code paths that share the same equation of motion in the radical-pair sector by construction. It does not constitute a test of formalism-independence between structurally distinct recombination treatments.

(c) Absolute-rate scaling invariance is not supported. Amplitude varies by orders of magnitude across the tested λ range; peak location shifts.

(d) The *z*-dephasing channel acts only on electron-spin *z*-components, not isotropically (i.e., *S*_x and *S*_y dephasing are not included).

(e) The spin-flip relaxation channel acts only on electron 1 in the present implementation. Symmetric spin-flip relaxation on both electrons may behave differently and remains untested.

(f) Noise scans were performed at *u* = 0 rather than at the exact baseline peak *u** ≈ −0.3. Results characterize the response near, but not exactly at, the plateau center.

(g) Spectral diagnostics are correlative. The slowest-decay-rate proxy correlates strongly with anisotropy (*r* ≈ 0.91), but this is not a mechanistic proof of mode structure or a phase-transition statement.

(h) Three-radical scavenging chemistry, anisotropic relaxation channels, and explicit cryptochrome-relevant geometric constraints are outside the scope of this work.

(i) Toy-model and 3D-geometry exploratory simulations are reported elsewhere as exploratory work and are not primary evidence for the claims made here.

## 6. Conclusion

A reduced radical-pair model with axial hyperfine coupling, geomagnetic-scale field, Fibonacci-sphere orientation sampling, and validated yield closure exhibits a reproducible broad response peak in recombination-ratio space at *u** ≈ −0.3 with FWHM ≈ 1.2 decades. The response requires hyperfine anisotropy, depends on absolute kinetic scale, is enhanced by *z*-dephasing in a finite window, is suppressed by single-electron spin-flip relaxation, and correlates with the slowest-decay-rate Liouvillian spectral proxy at *r* ≈ 0.91. These findings are bounded to the tested implementation; the public reproducibility archive supports independent verification of every numerical claim. The work establishes a claim-aligned reduced-model baseline against which broader extensions (full cryptochrome systems, fully independent recombination formalisms, isotropic dephasing, symmetric spin-flip relaxation on both electrons, polymorphic response morphologies) can be tested.
